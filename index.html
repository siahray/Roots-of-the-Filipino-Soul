Painting · Sculpture · Art

Canvases of Memory & Resistance

From ancient burial jars to revolutionary paintings — Filipino art as witness, testimony, and protest.

Metal Age · ~5 BC – AD 370

The Sculpted Faces of the Dead — Maitum Jars

Discovered in 1991 inside Ayub Cave in Maitum, Sarangani Province, these earthenware secondary burial jars date to the Metal Age. The lids are sculpted as human figures, capturing distinct facial expressions believed to represent the specific individuals buried inside.

These anthropomorphic vessels are a profound archaeological treasure. They shatter the misconception that pre-colonial Filipinos lacked sophisticated culture or art, providing concrete material evidence of a thriving society with a highly complex system of animistic beliefs, deep reverence for the afterlife, and exceptional pottery craftsmanship — long before any foreign influence reached our shores.

Standing before the Maitum Jars was arguably the most striking moment of the visit. It is one thing to read about "pre-colonial ancestors," but looking directly into the sculpted clay faces of people who lived thousands of years ago made our history feel incredibly tangible. This encounter fundamentally shifted my perspective, reminding me that the Philippine story did not begin in 1521 — it was already flourishing in the hands of the potters of Sarangani.

Colonial Era · 1719

The Assassination of Governor Bustamante

Painted by Félix Resurrección Hidalgo, this masterpiece illustrates the chaotic murder of Spanish Governor-General Bustamante in 1719 — when a violent mob of friars stormed the Palacio del Gobernador.

This painting is a vital visual document of the Frailocracia (Friarocracy) in Spanish colonial Philippines. It boldly illustrates a critical power dynamic: the Catholic Church often wielded more absolute, untouchable power than the Spanish crown's appointed government officials, exposing corruption, political dominance, and violence shadowing religious institutions of the era.

This painting completely arrested my attention because of its sheer brutality — especially since the perpetrators of the violence are figures traditionally associated with peace and holiness. It changed my perspective by stripping away the romanticized version of the Spanish colonial era, revealing the harsh political realities our ancestors lived under. It serves as a striking reminder that preserving our history means confronting the uncomfortable truths about who truly held power over the Filipino people.

1884 · National Consciousness

Spoliarium — Detail View

Juan Luna's colossal Spoliarium won the First Gold Medal at the Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes in Madrid. It depicts the bloody basement of the Roman Colosseum where dead gladiators were stripped of their armor — widely recognized as an allegory for Filipino suffering under Spanish rule.

Jose Rizal himself interpreted Luna's work as a reflection of the "social, moral, and political life" of the Philippines — a nation stripped of its dignity and dragged through the darkness of oppression. It is a masterpiece that helped spark the Filipino national consciousness.

Encountering even a fragment of the Spoliarium is deeply moving. The sheer scale and visceral use of color convey an overwhelming sense of tragedy and injustice. What struck me most was how art can be weaponized for advocacy — Luna didn't paint a Philippine scene, yet every Filipino saw their own suffering in the dragged gladiators.

1947 · Battle of Manila

Echoes of the Occupation — "Rape and Massacre in Ermita"

Painted in 1947 by Diosdado Lorenzo, this work documents the atrocities committed during the Battle of Manila in February 1945, when retreating Japanese forces unleashed violence upon the civilian population of the Ermita district.

Lorenzo's work deliberately shifts the historical lens away from grand, glorified narratives of soldiers and generals, forcing the viewer to confront the intimate, devastating suffering of the common Filipino. Using a raw, expressionistic style with heavy impasto, he captures the visceral terror and chaos of the massacre — ensuring the trauma inflicted upon the Filipino people is never erased from our historical consciousness.

Standing in front of this painting struck a completely different, deeply unsettling chord. Knowing that this painting depicts events from 1945 — the era of our grandparents and great-grandparents — made the history feel incredibly close. It is horrifying to realize that the familiar streets of Manila were sites of such unspeakable trauma.

Bert Monterona · Mindanao

"Mindanao Historical Struggle"

Created by Bert Monterona, this vibrant tapestry captures the complex, centuries-long history of the southern Philippines — visually narrating the fierce resistance of Lumads and Moros against various waves of colonization, exploitation, and marginalization.

This artwork powerfully challenges the often Luzon-centric narrative of Philippine history. It highlights the fierce, continuous resistance of Mindanao communities who successfully defended their ancestral lands and Islamic or indigenous beliefs against Spanish conquistadors. Monterona's tapestry serves as a visual celebration of rich cultural heritage, martial prowess, and unyielding spirit.

What drew me to this tapestry was its explosion of color and the unapologetic pride radiating from the indigenous figures. Too often, our history books focus heavily on how the northern parts were conquered, but this exhibit tells the story of how fiercely the South fought back. It made me incredibly proud of our diverse martial heritage.

Maranao Okir · Lanao

Maranao Okir and the Sarimanok

This display features the high-art tradition of the Maranao people of Lanao — prominently the Panolong (carved house-beams) and ritual objects featuring the Sarimanok, a legendary bird symbolizing good fortune and prestige, historically found in the Torogan (royal houses).

The art shown is a prime example of Okir — a design language characterized by flowing, curvilinear lines and S-shapes, heavily influenced by Islamic aesthetics. This collection represents the Sovereignty of the South: it proves that the Philippines has a "royal" history of sultanates, complex maritime trade, and a decorative style that is among the most sophisticated in Southeast Asia.

Standing before these dark, elegant carvings, I felt like I was witnessing the "Golden Age" of a Philippine kingdom. The sweeping curves of the Sarimanok are so dynamic — they look like they are in constant motion. This piece challenged my perspective by showing me that our national identity is also about grandeur; we have a tradition of "royalty" and high art that is entirely our own.

"Our cultural heritage is as much about our capacity to create profound art as it is about our resilience."
